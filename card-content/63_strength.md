# Meaning

Solitude can feel like a quiet room — or like an empty one. The difference lies in how we meet it. Many of us are taught, subtly or loudly, to avoid being alone. To fill the silence. To stay in motion. Somewhere along the way, we may have learned that being alone means being unloved, unsuccessful, or unsafe. But solitude, when chosen and embraced, can become a sanctuary.

Time alone creates space for deep listening — not to others, but to yourself. Without the pull of performance or approval, your truth has a chance to rise. This is where clarity grows. Where inner resources become visible. It isn't always comfortable. Loneliness may show up, along with buried grief or forgotten desires. But sitting with yourself — truly sitting — builds strength from the inside out. It's not about retreating from life, but about returning to it with more presence. In that space, resilience unfolds quietly, like a tree deepening its roots.

# Question

What do you believe about being alone? Where did those beliefs come from? And what might change if solitude became a space of power instead of discomfort?

# Action

Gift yourself a moment of honest solitude. No distractions, no purpose — just space. Sit, walk, or lie down somewhere quiet and ask: What part of me speaks only when it's quiet enough to hear? Notice what arises — even if it's uncomfortable. Especially then. Be gentle. This is not about doing solitude perfectly — it's about showing up for yourself without noise.
